§ 1

Why Two People Get Different Results

⏱ 17 min

The gap between a useless AI response and a brilliant one almost never comes down to which model you use. It comes down to how you ask. That skill has a name: prompt engineering.

Prompt Engineering
Crafting your input to an AI model to maximize the quality, relevance, and usefulness of the output. Not tricking the AI — communicating clearly with it.
💬
Analogy · The Vague Match Report

"The team lost" vs "India's death-over economy rate spiked to 12.4 across the last 5 T20s, and the opposition is scoring 60% of their total in the last 5 overs — check if our yorker execution is breaking down under pressure." Both describe the same defeat. One gets a useful answer in ten minutes. The other wastes a day just decoding what you meant.

§ 2

  
The RCTFC Framework

  
⏱ 20 min

  
What made Prompt B better? A five-part structure that works for every prompt you'll ever write: Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints.


  

    
🍽️

    
Analogy · Ordering at a Dhaba

    
"Give me food" vs "the grilled paneer tikka (Task), medium spice (Constraint), rice not naan (Format), I'm allergic to nuts (Context), and I trust your chef's seasoning (Role)." Same kitchen, wildly different meal. AI responds to specificity the same way.

  

  

    
🏏

    
Second Analogy · Setting the Batting Order

    
Tell a junior analyst "sort out the batsmen" and who knows what you get. Tell them: "night chase under lights, heavy dew (Context), as the senior batting analyst (Role), finalize the top 6 (Task), numbered list with one reason each (Format), don't stack more than two left-handers back to back (Constraint)." Same five ingredients, same jump in usefulness.

  


  

    
RCTFC Framework

    A five-part structure for effective prompts: Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints. Together they give the AI everything it needs to produce exactly what you want.

Danger case — contradictory constraints: Meera writes Task: "include the full over-by-over commentary of the death overs" and Constraint: "under 500 words." The model can't do both — it either blows past the word limit or silently drops the commentary, and you won't know which without checking. RCTFC only helps when its five elements agree with each other.


  
Beginner note: you don't need all five elements every time. For "what does a googly do to the ball?", just the Task is enough. For anything needing more than a one-line answer, RCTFC is your go-to.

  
Pro note: the five elements don't have to appear in R-C-T-F-C order. Some of the best prompts open with the Task and close with Constraints. What matters is that all five show up somewhere — not the sequence.

  
Coming in Class 3: today, all five RCTFC elements live inside one prompt you retype every time. In production they get split up — Role + Context + Constraints get frozen once into a persistent system prompt that survives across every request, while only Task + Format change per call. Why that split exists, and what breaks when teams get it wrong, is where Class 3 starts.

§ 3

  
Few-Shot Prompting + Output Formatting

  
⏱ 30 min

  
RCTFC gives structure. But when output must match a very specific format — your committee's template — instructions alone can't capture the nuance. Instead of describing the format, you SHOW it.


  
🧢
Analogy · Coaching the Debutant

  
Telling a debutant bowler "bowl good yorkers" is zero-shot — he's guessing what "good" means. Showing him footage of three senior bowlers executing yorkers in the death overs and saying "like this" is few-shot. Same bowler, dramatically better first attempt.

  
🎙️
Second Analogy · Commentary Style Transfer

  
You can't fully describe "more energy, less dry stats" in one sentence — but play a clip of a commentator who does exactly that and say "more like this," and the model gets it instantly. Few-shot prompting is the same move: stop describing the style, show it.


  

    Zero-Shot = instructions only, no examples
    Few-Shot = 2–3 examples + the task
  


  
Danger case — inconsistent examples: Meera grabs 3 old action-item messages fast — one uses "Owner:", another "Assigned to:", a third skips priority entirely. Few-shot with inconsistent examples doesn't average them into something clean — it confuses the model into blending fields unpredictably. Bad examples are worse than no examples.

Advanced note: more examples isn't always better. Past ~5 examples, the model can start overfitting to quirks of your specific examples rather than the general pattern. 2–3 well-chosen examples is the sweet spot.


  

    
Quiz 2

    
Your franchise has a specific Player Report Card format with sections like "Role & Style," "Recent Form," "Fitness Status," and "Risk Flags" in a very particular style. You want the AI to produce one in exactly this format. Which approach is most effective?

    
    
    
    
    
Why B? When output must match a specific template, showing beats telling. You don't hand a new teammate a 20-page style guide — you show them three great report cards and say "do it like this."

  


  
Few-shot solved the template problem for one audience. But Meera's notes need to reach four different people — a WhatsApp update, a selection table, a JSON payload for the analytics tool, and a formal player report. Same information, four shapes. That's output formatting.


  
Output Formatting

  Explicitly defining the structure, shape, and style of the AI's response. Like specifying exact scorecard columns instead of "give me the match data" — you get exactly what you asked for.


  
🏟️
Analogy · The Scorecard Contract

  
You wouldn't accept a scorecard missing overs bowled or strike rate — a scorecard has a contract: these fields, in this order, every time. Output formatting is that same rigor applied to a chat model instead of a printed sheet.


  
Danger case — over-specified format: Meera defines a JSON schema with 20 required fields for a 3-line WhatsApp update. The source notes only support 6 of them. Rather than leaving fields blank, the model invents plausible-sounding values to fill the schema. Format control only helps when the shape matches the substance you actually have.


  
Player Report Card

  A short, standardized document describing a player — role, recent form, strengths/weaknesses, fitness status — so any selector can understand what a player brings before relying on them.

§ 4

  
Chain-of-Thought Reasoning

  
⏱ 13 min

  
The coach's follow-up isn't a formatting ask — it's a reasoning ask. Some tasks need the AI to actually think through a problem, not just organize text that's already there.


  

    
✖️Warm-Up · What Does "Chain of Thought" Even Mean?

    

      
Forget cricket for a second. Take the simplest possible problem: 23 × 47 = ? Watch the difference between blurting an answer and working it out.

      

        

          
😬 Straight to the answer (no CoT)

          
23 × 47 = 1081

          
Might be right, might not — there's nothing to check. If a mental slip happened somewhere, it's buried inside one number.

        

        

          
🧠 Step by step (CoT)

          
Step 1 — Split the harder number: 47 = 40 + 7
Step 2 — Solve the easy piece: 23 × 40 = 920
Step 3 — Solve the leftover piece: 23 × 7  = 161
Step 4 — Add the pieces back: 920 + 161 = 1081

          
Same final answer — but every step is checkable. If Step 3 had said 23 × 7 = 151, you'd catch it right there instead of trusting a wrong 1071.

        

      

      
That's the whole idea, no cricket required: chain-of-thought means don't jump straight to the final answer — make the model lay out its intermediate steps first. It works for splitting a two-digit multiplication. It works exactly the same way when the "problem" is a coach asking which risk is most urgent — just with bigger, messier steps instead of arithmetic.

    

  


  
🎯
Analogy · The Captain's Field Chat

  
Before setting an unusual field, a good captain doesn't just blurt "third man out, mid-on in." He talks it through with the bowler out loud — pitch conditions, the batter's scoring zones, overs left. Saying it out loud catches gaps before the ball is bowled. Chain-of-thought is the same move: telling the model to think step by step surfaces reasoning errors before you see the final answer.

  
🧮
Second Analogy · Working Out the Revised Target

  
After a rain interruption, nobody just guesses the target. You write it out: overs remaining, wickets in hand, par score, revised target. Skip a step and you get it wrong. An AI is no different — force it to externalize intermediate steps and the odds of a correct final answer go up a lot.


  
Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting

  Explicitly asking the AI to reason through a problem step by step before its final answer. The model "shows its work" — and that process produces better results.


  
Danger case — CoT on a task that doesn't need it: ask "think step by step" before "what does a googly do to the ball?" and you get a paragraph of unnecessary deliberation before a one-line answer — slower, and sometimes the reasoning talks the model into second-guessing a correct instinct. Reserve CoT for tasks that actually require multiple reasoning steps.


  

    
🔗SIM 4 · Chain-of-Thought Stepper

    

      
Task: "Which open risk is most urgent to flag to the coach?"

      

        

          
😓 Direct Answer (no CoT)

          
- Unresolved form-metric debate
- Trials blocked on missing probable-30 list
- No rollback plan if new XI underperforms

          
Plausible but shallow. No ranking, no reasoning for why any one is "most urgent."

        

        

          
🧠 Chain-of-Thought Answer

          
          
          
    
            
  1. Step 1 — Map: Probable-30 (20 Jul), Batting model (25 Jul), Dashboard metrics (22 Jul), Domestic testing (no date), Rollback plan (undiscussed)
    2. 
            
  2. Step 2 — Dependency chains: Probable-30 → Trials → Batting model → Domestic testing → Final selection
    3. 
            
  3. Step 3 — Flag ownerless items: Recent-form-vs-career-average comparison doc has no owner and no deadline
    4. 
            
  4. Step 4 — Trace cascades: Probable-30 slippage on 20 Jul cascades through 4 downstream stages; the form-metric gap blocks a clean rationale for every pick after it
    5. 
            
  5. Step 5 — What's missing: A rollback plan was never discussed at all
    6. 
          

          
Final answer: the probable-30 list is the single highest-blast-radius risk — a slip on 20 Jul delays everything downstream. Second: the ownerless form-metric decision, since nobody is accountable for unblocking it.

        

      

    

  


  
The steps are visible in the output — CoT didn't just relabel the same list, it produced a ranked, reasoned answer no direct prompt gave us.

  
Rule of thumb: if a good answer requires more than one mental step, add chain-of-thought. If you'd huddle and talk it through before deciding, tell the AI to huddle and talk it through too.

  
Beginner note: the simplest way to trigger CoT is the literal phrase "think step by step" before your question. Try it once on any multi-step question — you'll see the difference immediately.

  
In the industry: Anthropic's Claude was one of the first models to bake this in natively — they call it "extended thinking." The technique you just used by hand is the same one, just automated.

§ 5

  
Refining a Prompt in Five Rounds

  
⏱ 17 min

  
Almost nobody writes a great prompt on the first try. Prompt refinement IS debugging: write, read the output, identify the failure, fix one thing, run again.


  
🥅
Analogy · Facing the Nets, Not the Match

  
No batsman walks into a net session expecting the first ball to be perfect. You face a ball, adjust your foot position, face the next, adjust your backlift — one change per ball, not a random reshuffle of your whole technique. A prompt improves the same way: one deliberate fix per round.

Meera's output is factually correct and well-structured, but the tone is too formal for an internal WhatsApp update — it reads like a press release. What is the most effective next step?

§ 7

  
Wrap-Up — The Prompt Engineer's Checklist

  
⏱ 5 min

  
Five takeaways to reference every time you write a prompt.


  
1
RCTFC anatomy
Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints. Same principle as a well-written team selection brief: specificity in, quality out.

  
2
Zero-shot vs few-shot
When output must match a template, show 2–3 examples. The examples ARE the instruction.

  
3
Chain-of-thought
For analysis or multi-step reasoning, tell the AI to think step by step. The captain's field chat, applied to AI.

  
4
Output formatting
Define output like a scorecard: exact fields, headers, order. If you can sketch it in 10 seconds, the sketch belongs in the prompt.

  
5
Iterative refinement
Prompting is debugging, not dictation. Write, read, diagnose, fix one thing, repeat. Nobody ships their first net session as match technique.
